# 🎯 Topic Connection

This activity explores **mental wellbeing and technology** by helping participants reflect on the difference between the identity they present to the world—especially online—and how they truly feel inside.

Through **creative writing, drawing, and mark-making**, participants use art as a tool for emotional expression rather than artistic performance. The activity highlights how digital spaces and social media often encourage carefully controlled self-presentation, which can contribute to pressure, shame, loneliness, or emotional disconnection.

By intentionally switching from the dominant hand to the non-dominant hand, participants experience letting go of perfection and explore a more authentic, vulnerable form of self-expression.

# 🧰 Materials Needed

- Printed feelings wheel (one per participant or several shared copies)
- A4 paper or notebooks
- Pens, pencils, or markers
- Timer
- Optional calm background music and speaker

---

# 📍 Preparation

Before participants arrive:

- Print the feelings wheel.
- Arrange a quiet space where everyone can sit comfortably.
- Prepare paper and writing materials.
- Create a calm and safe atmosphere.

---

# 📖 Activity Description

## Step 1 – Introduction *(1–2 minutes)*

Welcome participants and explain that this is a personal reflection activity.

Remind everyone that:

- No artistic skills are required.
- There are no right or wrong answers.
- Sharing is completely voluntary.
- Papers do not need to be shown to anyone.

Create a safe and respectful atmosphere.

---

## Step 2 – Guided Meditation *(5 minutes)*

Invite participants to sit comfortably.

Guide them to:

- Focus on their breathing.
- Notice sensations in their body.
- Become aware of the surrounding environment.
- Observe any emotions present without trying to change them.

Keep the tone slow, calm, and supportive.

---

## Step 3 – Feelings Wheel *(1–2 minutes)*

Provide or display the printed feelings wheel.

Ask participants to quietly choose **one emotion** that best reflects how they currently feel.

Remind them that there is no perfect answer.

---

## Step 4 – Dominant Hand Reflection *(5–7 minutes)*

Participants write or draw using **their dominant hand**.

Prompt:

> **"How do I present myself?"**

Encourage them to think about:

- Social media
- Online spaces
- School or work
- Friends
- Family
- The image they intentionally present to others

Participants may:

- Write words
- Write sentences
- Draw
- Create symbols
- Make abstract marks

---

## Step 5 – Non-Dominant Hand Reflection *(5–7 minutes)*

Participants use **their non-dominant hand** on a new sheet or the reverse side.

Prompt:

> **"How do I present myself in reality, when I am not trying to look perfect?"**

Encourage them to embrace imperfect handwriting or drawing.

Explain that slowing down often allows different thoughts and emotions to emerge.

---

## Step 6 – Quiet Comparison *(2–3 minutes)*

Participants look at both pages.

Invite them to:

- Circle a word.
- Underline a sentence.
- Highlight a symbol.
- Notice anything that feels meaningful or surprising.

Encourage silent personal reflection.

---

## Step 7 – Sharing in Pairs *(5–7 minutes)*

Participants discuss the activity in pairs.

Suggested prompts:

- How did it feel using each hand?
- What differences did you notice?
- Did anything surprise you?
- Did the second version feel more honest?

Participants decide for themselves how much they wish to share.

---

## Step 8 – Group Reflection *(4–5 minutes)*

Bring everyone back together.

Invite short reflections using questions such as:

- How was the process?
- What differences did you notice between the two versions?
- Was choosing an emotion easy or difficult?
- Did the second reflection feel more authentic?
- What does this activity reveal about how we present ourselves online versus in real life?
- Do you feel more connected to yourself or others after this activity?

# 🎯 Objectives

This activity aims to:

- Explore the difference between how participants present themselves online, in everyday life, and how they genuinely feel.
- Use creative writing, drawing, and mark-making as accessible artistic tools for emotional expression.
- Develop emotional literacy by helping participants identify and name emotions using the feelings wheel.
- Encourage reflection on how technology, perfectionism, shame, and fear of judgement influence self-presentation.
- Foster authentic connection by creating a safe environment where participants can share experiences and recognize that they are not alone in their emotions.

---

# 💬 Debriefing Questions

- How was the process for you?
- What differences did you notice between using your dominant hand and your non-dominant hand?
- Was it easy or difficult to choose an emotion from the feelings wheel?
- Did the second version feel more honest, more uncomfortable, or more connected to reality?
- What does this activity reveal about the way we present ourselves online and in real life?
- Do you feel more connected to yourself or to others after this activity?

# 🧘 Guided Meditation Script

> *Find a comfortable position. You can close your eyes or simply lower your gaze.*
>
> *Take a slow breath in... and a slow breath out.*
>
> *Notice your feet, your hands, your shoulders, and the space around you.*
>
> *You do not need to change anything. Simply notice what is already here.*
>
> *Perhaps you feel calm, tired, pressure, shame, joy, confusion, or something else.*
>
> *Allow that feeling to exist without judging it.*
>
> *In a moment, you will choose a word for this feeling and gently place it on paper.*

---

# 🛡️ Safety & Inclusion Notes

- Sharing is always voluntary.
- Participants may keep their work private.
- Facilitators should never interpret or analyse anyone's drawing or writing.
- Participants may write in any language or communicate through symbols.
- If language skills are limited, facilitators may demonstrate using simple drawings or symbols.
- If possible, provide translated versions of the feelings wheel or encourage participants to help one another understand unfamiliar emotions.

---

# 🌱 Closing Reflection

> *The goal was never to create a perfect drawing or piece of writing.*
>
> *The goal was simply to notice what happens when we stop controlling the image for a moment.*
>
> *Sometimes genuine connection begins when we allow the less perfect version of ourselves to exist.*

---

# 💡 Learning Outcomes

After completing this activity, participants will be able to:

- Recognize differences between public identity and private emotions.
- Use art and creative expression as tools for emotional awareness.
- Identify and name emotions with greater confidence.
- Reflect on the influence of technology and social media on self-image and mental wellbeing.
- Practice authenticity, vulnerability, empathy, and self-compassion.
