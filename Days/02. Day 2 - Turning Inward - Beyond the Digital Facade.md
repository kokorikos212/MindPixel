![Day 2 Overview](assets/slides/day2.jpeg)

Yesterday, we learned each other's names. Today, we started discovering the people behind them.
The second day invited us to slow down, reflect, and begin exploring the themes that would accompany us throughout the project. Through creative activities, discussion and personal reflection, we looked beyond first impressions and started asking bigger questions—not only about the digital world around us, but also about ourselves.

(Session 1) We started the second day by getting to know the programme that had brought us all together. Through an interactive introduction to Erasmus+ and Youthpass, we explored the opportunities these programmes offer and how learning often happens outside the classroom.

Working in small groups, we also discovered the eight Youthpass competences, discussing what they mean in practice and how we might develop them throughout the exchange. Rather than seeing Youthpass as just a certificate, we began looking at it as a way to recognise our own growth—one experience, challenge and reflection at a time.

(Session 2) 
The next session invited us to pause and reflect before diving deeper into the project. Using coloured sticky notes, we individually wrote down our expectations, fears, and the contributions we hoped to bring to the group. Once all the notes found their place on a shared board, we explored them together.

It quickly became clear that many of us were carrying similar hopes and worries. Reading them aloud created an atmosphere of honesty and reassurance, reminding us that we weren't facing this journey alone. At the same time, seeing the wide variety of skills, experiences and ideas people were ready to contribute highlighted just how much we could learn from one another. It was a simple activity that helped align the group and gave everyone a sense of ownership from the very beginning.

(Session 3)

Our World Café was the first opportunity to dive into the project's main topics. Divided into small groups, we rotated between different discussion tables, each exploring a question from a different perspective. Every conversation built upon the previous group's ideas, gradually transforming blank sheets of paper into colourful maps of thoughts, experiences and debates.

Rather than searching for the "correct" answer, the session encouraged curiosity. Some discussions challenged our assumptions, others confirmed shared experiences, and many reminded us how differently people perceive the same issue depending on their background and daily life. By the end, every participant had left a piece of their perspective on each table, creating something far richer than any individual could have produced alone.

The four discussion questions were:

How does social media affect us in everyday life?
What should youth workers do to encourage the responsible use of technology among young people?
How is Artificial Intelligence affecting our productivity and creativity?
What is art, and how can it be used to improve our mental wellbeing?


(Session 4)

We ended the day's programme with a Mood Collage. Participants explored their own social media feeds, collecting posts, images and visual elements that triggered an immediate emotional response. These were then transformed into personal collages, creating a visual snapshot of how our digital environments influence the way we feel.

More than anything, this session was our first introduction to art as a tool for reflection. It showed us that creativity doesn't have to be perfect to be meaningful and that even something as simple as a collage can help us understand ourselves a little better. As every day, we closed with a reflection. It gave everyone a chance to share thoughts, emotions, and small discoveries from the day, while helping us slow down and appreciate how much we had already experienced together.
