(Session 1) Our first morning was all about getting to know one another. Through a series of name games, we slowly transformed a room full of strangers into a group that could already laugh together. Every activity added another layer of familiarity, making names easier to remember and conversations easier to start.

The name game that stayed with us the most asked everyone to introduce themselves with one intentionally boring fact. Ironically, the "boring" facts became the most memorable ones. From passionately hating plain milk to proudly claiming there was nothing interesting to say, these small details quickly turned into inside jokes. Repeating every name and fact after each person transformed a simple introduction into a surprisingly fun memory challenge.

The moment that filled the room with the most laughter came during our ball game. What began with one ball and calm concentration soon turned into joyful chaos as more balls were added. People were laughing, missing catches, helping each other and somehow still managing to learn everyone's names.

We finished the session with speed dating, giving everyone the chance to have short but meaningful conversations with almost every participant. By the end of the morning, the room already felt different. The first names had been learned, the first jokes had been shared, and the first connections had quietly begun to grow.

(Session 2) 
With the first names learned and the initial awkwardness behind us, it was time to discover how we worked together. Through the classic NASA "Lost in Space" survival exercise, we were challenged to rank a list of items that would help us survive after crash-landing far from the mother ship.

The real challenge wasn't choosing the "right" answers—it was convincing others, listening to different perspectives, and finding common ground. Every participant arrived with their own logic, and lively discussions quickly filled the room as opinions changed, compromises were made, and unexpected leaders emerged.

The biggest realization came when the official NASA ranking was revealed. Some groups celebrated, others laughed at how confidently wrong they had been, but everyone walked away with the same lesson: teamwork isn't about everyone agreeing from the start. It's about communicating, trusting each other's ideas, and building a solution together.

(Session 3) 
After a short energiser, it was time for our first Mission Impossible. Working against the clock, each team had one hour to complete eighteen creative and sometimes completely unexpected challenges. There was no single strategy—only teamwork.

From recreating famous artworks with our bodies to designing badges, creating playlists, filming videos and even applauding ourselves at exactly minute 33, every task required something different. Somewhere along the way, we also uncovered the meaning of the mysterious acronym CCDC: Collaboration, Communication, Delegation, Creativity —a joke that stayed with us for the rest of the project.

The mission wasn't about finishing first. It was about discovering how different personalities naturally found their roles, supported one another, and turned a long list of random challenges into one shared adventure.

(Session 4) 
To close the day, we came together to create our own MindPixel Manifesto. Instead of being handed a list of rules, everyone contributed ideas about the kind of environment we wanted to build together. Through discussion, we agreed on values like active listening, open communication, respecting boundaries, contributing actively, and even our slightly mysterious rule: Bora Bora!

By creating the manifesto together, the rules stopped feeling like restrictions and became a shared commitment. Throughout the week, it remained on the wall as a reminder that the atmosphere of the project was something we were all responsible for creating.

(ICN 1 - LATVIA) 
Our first intercultural evening was hosted by Latvia. Alongside discovering Latvian culture, food and traditions, the evening also invited us to reflect on something more personal—our own boundaries.

Rather than treating the intercultural night as just a presentation, it became an opportunity to talk about what makes us feel comfortable, respected and safe within a group. It was a simple but meaningful way to understand one another better and set the foundation for the days ahead. By the end of the evening, we hadn't only learned more about Latvia—we had also learned how to better take care of one another.
