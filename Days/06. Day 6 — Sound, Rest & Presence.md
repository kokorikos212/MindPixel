Day 6

Activity 1: Photo Journal

In persuit of connection

About: Working individually or in pairs, we set out on a walk - some through the forest, others through the city, with one simple question: Where do we see connections? Was it in two cherries hanging from the same branch? An old clock that once brought a village together? Or in photographs of friendships and moments shared throughout the project?

What happened: Over the course of about 90 minutes, we captured images of nature, people, places, and symbols that reflected what connection means to each of us. We paired our photographs with short texts and poems, creating a series of collages.

Later, we came back together to present our collages and share the stories behind them. 

The most inspiring part was seeing how differently everyone interpreted the same prompt and saw connection in different little things. 

Quotes:

Milica: The most impactful for me was photo journaling activity. The topic was connection, so it made me think about all the connection we form in Erasmus, and how that impacts our lives afterwards. It made me appriciate the passing connections even more, and it pulled me closer to myself, nature and other people.

  
  

Activity 2: 3in1 workshops with Jēkabs, Luna and Sophie  
We were inspired by how proactive some of the participants were, preparing three short workshops for the group and offering a gentle introduction to the next day's activities.

Sophie opened the session with an association card and storytelling exercise. Each group of four participants took turns contributing a single word inspired by a card, gradually weaving these words into funny and introspective stories. The exercise lasted around 15–20 minutes and filled the room with laughter while helping us connect with one another.

Luna then invited us to reconnect with ourselves through a short guided meditation. Afterwards, we explored our emotions by representing them as colours on an outline of our bodies. The final part of her workshop was a dialogue between our left and right brain hemispheres: using our dominant hand, we wrote reflective questions, and with our non-dominant hand, we wrote the answers.

Jēkabs guided us through an exercise that combined visual, spatial, and auditory expression. Armed with five blank sheets of paper, markers, a list of six basic emotions, and a carefully selected playlist of five very different songs, we explored how music evokes feelings within us and then translated those emotions into abstract drawings. We loved to see how differently each person interpreted the same music and emotions, celebrating individual perspectives.

Playlist used during Jēkabs' workshop: [Emotions - playlist by Jēkabs Silanžs | Spotify](https://open.spotify.com/playlist/3lxBRBCBzASRrmUni3jqmX?si=MiovOxHRS_Oo8TeEKt7aVg&utm_source=copy-link&pi=Wj6Z5gQ5TN68A&nd=1&dlsi=d4221becb6214952) (+ [5th song](https://youtu.be/i31h7A2fRzI?is=XbchBq1PS-hebBtH))

Jēkabs' insight about the workshop:

Creating my workshop was easy and exciting because it was inspired by several activities from last year's training course (MindSketch), as well as personal experiences that inspired the song choices and the feeling of nostalgia -- I had a strong emotional connection to what I had on my mind. 

Last year a similar activity about visualising one's emotions truly opened up my creativity and helped me to process and discern my emotions and feelings. It kickstarted the journey of unlocking my potential for conceptualising and creating something that might be considered art. I wanted to share this experience with this year's participants, so it would be easier for them to connect with their emotions, put them in words and visualizations, in order to take their next step towards opening up and creating something they might be proud about, as I have been quite a few times since the last year's project. 

(P. S. I have used this activity with my teenage students before reading and writing poetry to better understand abstract characters and thoughts. Thus, there are many ways of applying and adjusting it.)

Activity 3: Boredom meditation

About: Participants were encouraged to explore boredom and afterwards use their feelings, or thoughts to express themselves creatively. 

What happened: The active part of the session lasted for 45 minutes; the participants had to put their phones away and stay in silence, they were not permitted to leave the room and to disturb others. The results varied between creative writing, drawing, doodling, engaging into performance art and getting in touch with their body, or emotions. 

Quotes:

Kamilla: Turning away from participants, I fixed my eye on a white wall and found it comfortable. It was cleaning me, my mind of the emotional garbage I got from others, as the first tear fell, and then another, I lost control. I was surrounded, caught between the fear of staying and the dread of leaving.  So I became statue. Yet, through it all, I wasn't bored for a moment. I’m never bored with living.

Aigly: I am surprised how everyone let themselves be free once they leaned into it and accepted it.

Luna: The boredom activity brought a lot to my awareness about my own fidgeting reactions and about how other people work with their boredom state. It showed me how we can use boredom as a starter into creative activities, but also how different we all are in how we manage our energy and creativity.

Nader: The boredom session was the most impactful. I liked observing people being bored and how some of them allowed themselves to sit with it and others didn't allow one minute of listening to themselves. For me, I started to think that I have a decent tolerance for boredom. Maybe I can integrate it in little daily sessions.
